**Written form <MNG links="W" />**. Written form <MNG links="W" /> is used in Hudum letter <MNG links="ee" /> and <MNG links="w" />, but has different origin in these two letters, since the form <MNG links="W" /> in <MNG links="ee" /> comes from Tibetan letter _e_, and the form <MNG links="W" /> in <MNG links="w" /> comes from _beth_.

## Phonetic letters

The variants in gray indicate the fabricated variants, _i.e._ variants borrowed from other joining positions. The variants in yellow indicate the variants that are unattested in the modern orthography of Traditional Mongolian.

<LetterTable locale={locale} />

**Isolated <MNG links="Ix" /> and isolated <MNG links="Ux" />**. Isolated <MNG links="Ix" /> and isolated <MNG links="Ux" /> are used historically, with examples shown in [(Dorji _et al._, 2017)](https://www.unicode.org/L2/L2017/17035r2-mongolian-std-var.pdf). In some Traditional Mongolian literature, even if the letter <MNG links="n" /> before isolated <MNG links="Ix" /> or <MNG links="Ux" /> is omitted, the left point is retained and therefore has to be consistently analyzed as belonging to <MNG links="i" />, <MNG links="u" /> or <MNG links="ue" />.

**Final <MNG links="I" /> in letter <MNG links="j" />**. Final <MNG links="I" /> in letter <MNG links="j" /> is introduced in [(Dorji _et al._, 2017)](https://www.unicode.org/L2/L2017/17035r2-mongolian-std-var.pdf), where the written form of <MNG links="j" /> in the example <MNG links="b u i – j · a" /> is not final, but isolated. Since there are currently no examples exist to prove its existence, the written form is marked in gray, but the shaping process still supports the theoretical existence of a word with final <MNG links="I" /> in letter <MNG links="j" /> and chachlag.

**Medial <MNG links="I I" /> in letter <MNG links="y" /> and medial <MNG links="O" /> in letter <MNG links="w" />**. Medial <MNG links="I I" /> in letter <MNG links="y" /> and medial <MNG links="O" /> in letter <MNG links="w" /> are introduced in [(Dorji _et al._, 2017)](https://www.unicode.org/L2/L2017/17035r2-mongolian-std-var.pdf), where the written form <MNG links="I I" /> of letter <MNG links="y" /> in the examples <MNG links="a y l" /> and <MNG links="a y m a g" />, and the written form <MNG links="O" /> of letter <MNG links="w" /> in the example <MNG links="t a w l a y" /> are all differences derived from letter analysis. Since <MNG links="a i l" />, <MNG links="a i m a g" /> and <MNG links="t a u l a i" /> are preferred, these written forms are marked in gray.

**Letter <MNG links="k2" /> (<MNG links="K2" />) and letter <MNG links="k" /> (<MNG links="K" />)**. Letter <MNG links="k2" /> in form <MNG links="K2" /> is used to represent /kʰ/ (Latin: k) in Hudum literature in China, while letter <MNG links="k" /> in form <MNG links="K" /> is used to represent /kʰ/ (Cyrillic: к) in Hudum literature in Mongolia. The reason for disunifying the two letters is that Hudum Ali Gali uses <MNG links="K2" /> to record /ɡ/ and uses <MNG links="K" /> to record /kʰ/.

**Medial <MNG links="Hr" /> in letter <MNG links="hh" /> and initial <MNG links="Zr" /> in letter <MNG links="zr" />**. Medial <MNG links="Hr" /> in letter <MNG links="hh" /> and initial <MNG links="Zr" /> in letter <MNG links="zr" /> are similar, so they are unified in proposals such as [(SAH, 2017)](https://www.unicode.org/L2/L2017/17035r2-mongolian-std-var.pdf). The reason for disunifying the two written units is that Hudum Ali Gali uses <MNG links="Hr" /> to record /ɦ/ and to indicate aspiration, and uses <MNG links="Zr" /> to record /ʈ/.
