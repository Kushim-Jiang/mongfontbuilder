**Written form <Mong locale="MNG" links="W" />**. Written form <Mong locale="MNG" links="W" /> is used in Hudum letter <Mong locale="MNG" links="ee" /> and <Mong locale="MNG" links="w" />, but has different origin in these two letters, since the form <Mong locale="MNG" links="W" /> in <Mong locale="MNG" links="ee" /> comes from Tibetan letter _e_, and the form <Mong locale="MNG" links="W" /> in <Mong locale="MNG" links="w" /> comes from _beth_.

## Phonetic letters

The variants in gray indicate the fabricated variants, _i.e._ variants borrowed from other joining positions. The variants in yellow indicate the variants that are unattested in the modern orthography of Traditional Mongolian.

<LetterTable locale={locale} />

**Isolated <Mong locale="MNG" links="Ix" /> and isolated <Mong locale="MNG" links="Ux" />**. Isolated <Mong locale="MNG" links="Ix" /> and isolated <Mong locale="MNG" links="Ux" /> are used historically, with examples shown in [(Dorji _et al._, 2017)](https://www.unicode.org/L2/L2017/17035r2-mongolian-std-var.pdf). In some Traditional Mongolian literature, even if the letter <Mong locale="MNG" links="n" /> before isolated <Mong locale="MNG" links="Ix" /> or <Mong locale="MNG" links="Ux" /> is omitted, the left point is retained and therefore has to be consistently analyzed as belonging to <Mong locale="MNG" links="i" />, <Mong locale="MNG" links="u" /> or <Mong locale="MNG" links="ue" />.

**Final <Mong locale="MNG" links="I" /> in letter <Mong locale="MNG" links="j" />**. Final <Mong locale="MNG" links="I" /> in letter <Mong locale="MNG" links="j" /> is introduced in [(Dorji _et al._, 2017)](https://www.unicode.org/L2/L2017/17035r2-mongolian-std-var.pdf), where the written form of <Mong locale="MNG" links="j" /> in the example <Mong locale="MNG" links="b u i – j · a" /> is not final, but isolated. Since there are currently no examples exist to prove its existence, the written form is marked in gray, but the shaping process still supports the theoretical existence of a word with final <Mong locale="MNG" links="I" /> in letter <Mong locale="MNG" links="j" /> and chachlag.

**Medial <Mong locale="MNG" links="I I" /> in letter <Mong locale="MNG" links="y" /> and medial <Mong locale="MNG" links="O" /> in letter <Mong locale="MNG" links="w" />**. Medial <Mong locale="MNG" links="I I" /> in letter <Mong locale="MNG" links="y" /> and medial <Mong locale="MNG" links="O" /> in letter <Mong locale="MNG" links="w" /> are introduced in [(Dorji _et al._, 2017)](https://www.unicode.org/L2/L2017/17035r2-mongolian-std-var.pdf), where the written form <Mong locale="MNG" links="I I" /> of letter <Mong locale="MNG" links="y" /> in the examples <Mong locale="MNG" links="a y l" /> and <Mong locale="MNG" links="a y m a g" />, and the written form <Mong locale="MNG" links="O" /> of letter <Mong locale="MNG" links="w" /> in the example <Mong locale="MNG" links="t a w l a y" /> are all differences derived from letter analysis. Since <Mong locale="MNG" links="a i l" />, <Mong locale="MNG" links="a i m a g" /> and <Mong locale="MNG" links="t a u l a i" /> are preferred, these written forms are marked in gray.

**Letter <Mong locale="MNG" links="k2" /> (<Mong locale="MNG" links="K2" />) and letter <Mong locale="MNG" links="k" /> (<Mong locale="MNG" links="K" />)**. Letter <Mong locale="MNG" links="k2" /> in form <Mong locale="MNG" links="K2" /> is used to represent /kʰ/ (Latin: k) in Hudum literature in China, while letter <Mong locale="MNG" links="k" /> in form <Mong locale="MNG" links="K" /> is used to represent /kʰ/ (Cyrillic: к) in Hudum literature in Mongolia. The reason for disunifying the two letters is that Hudum Ali Gali uses <Mong locale="MNG" links="K2" /> to record /ɡ/ and uses <Mong locale="MNG" links="K" /> to record /kʰ/.

**Medial <Mong locale="MNG" links="Hr" /> in letter <Mong locale="MNG" links="hh" /> and initial <Mong locale="MNG" links="Zr" /> in letter <Mong locale="MNG" links="zr" />**. Medial <Mong locale="MNG" links="Hr" /> in letter <Mong locale="MNG" links="hh" /> and initial <Mong locale="MNG" links="Zr" /> in letter <Mong locale="MNG" links="zr" /> are similar, so they are unified in proposals such as [(SAH, 2017)](https://www.unicode.org/L2/L2017/17035r2-mongolian-std-var.pdf). The reason for disunifying the two written units is that Hudum Ali Gali uses <Mong locale="MNG" links="Hr" /> to record /ɦ/ and to indicate aspiration, and uses <Mong locale="MNG" links="Zr" /> to record /ʈ/.
